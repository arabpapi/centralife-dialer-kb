# ARP+ Drill Bank

## How to Use This Bank

- ARP+ = **Acknowledge → Reframe → Pivot**
- Every sequence teaches the client something new. If your reframe just argues the objection, you're doing it wrong.
- Constructive tension: Hold the uncomfortable reality. Let the client sit in it. Don't rush to comfort.
- Each drill: Rep delivers the full ARP+ sequence out loud. Group scores: Did the reframe teach? Did the pivot redirect? Was tension present?

## Category 1: "I Already Have Insurance"

### 1A — Employer Coverage
**Client:** "I'm covered through work."  
**Acknowledge:** "That's good — it shows you're thinking about it."  
**Reframe:** "Here's what we're consistently seeing: employer coverage is typically 1–2x salary, which covers 12–24 months of income replacement. For most families, the actual need — mortgage, debts, income replacement, education — runs 5–10x salary. And employer coverage doesn't follow you if you leave or get let go. The gap between what's provided and what's required is usually significant."  
**Pivot:** "Let me show you the number based on your actual situation — then you'll know exactly where you stand."

### 1B — Old Personal Policy
**Client:** "I already have a policy."  
**Acknowledge:** "Smart — you were ahead of most people."  
**Reframe:** "The question isn't whether you have coverage — it's whether the amount still matches your life. When that policy was written, your income, mortgage, debts, and family may have looked completely different. Most people are insured for a version of their life that's 5–10 years old."  
**Pivot:** "Let me run the current numbers. If you're covered, I'll tell you and we're done in 10 minutes."

### 1C — Multiple Policies
**Client:** "I've got a couple of policies already."  
**Acknowledge:** "Good — that's more than most."  
**Reframe:** "The issue we see with multiple policies is often they were bought at different times for different reasons, and nobody's looked at the total architecture. One might overlap. One might have gaps in the riders. The death benefit total might still fall short of what the math requires. It's worth seeing the full picture in one view."  
**Pivot:** "What's the total face amount across everything? Let's put the number next to the need and see where it lands."

## Category 2: "I'm Fine / I Don't Need It"

### 2A — "We'd Be Fine"
**Client:** "Honestly, we'd figure it out."  
**Acknowledge:** "Most families feel that way."  
**Reframe:** "Here's what 'figuring it out' typically looks like: the surviving spouse absorbs 100% of the mortgage, the debts, and the income gap — all at once. Within 6–12 months, most families in that position are selling the house, relocating the kids, or draining retirement accounts at tax penalties. 'Figuring it out' has a price — we can calculate it."  
**Pivot:** "Let me walk through the math with your numbers. If the gap is small, you'll know. If it's not, you'll see exactly what it would take to close it."

### 2B — "I'm Young and Healthy"
**Client:** "I'm only 32, I'll deal with it later."  
**Acknowledge:** "That's actually the best time to address it."  
**Reframe:** "Two things are true right now that won't always be: your health qualifies you for the best rate class, and the cost is at its lowest. Every year you wait, the premium increases — and one health event can move you from preferred to rated or declined. The math doesn't care about age. The liabilities exist today."  
**Pivot:** "Let me show you what the rate looks like now vs. what it looks like in 5 or 10 years. The difference is usually enough to end the debate."

### 2C — "Insurance Is a Waste of Money"
**Client:** "I just think it's a waste."  
**Acknowledge:** "That's a common perspective."  
**Reframe:** "Insurance is the only financial instrument that creates an immediate estate — dollar one, day one. There's no other asset class that does that. A $500K policy at $60/month means your family gets $500,000 in exchange for $720/year. Every other asset requires decades of accumulation to reach equivalent liquidity. It's not a cost — it's a leverage ratio."  
**Pivot:** "Let me show you the leverage on your specific numbers. Once you see the ratio, the conversation shifts."

## Category 3: "I'm Busy / Not Now"

### 3A — Too Busy
**Client:** "I don't have time for this right now."  
**Acknowledge:** "I respect that — I'll keep this tight."  
**Reframe:** "Here's what I can tell you in 2 minutes: based on what I'm seeing, there's likely a gap between your current coverage and your actual liability. Most people have one and don't know it. The issue isn't time — it's that the gap exists whether we look at it or not. Addressing it takes one 20-minute conversation."  
**Pivot:** "Let me ask you three quick questions — if there's no gap, I'll tell you and we're done."

### 3B — "Call Me Back Later"
**Client:** "Just call me back next month."  
**Acknowledge:** "I can do that."  
**Reframe:** "The reason I'd prefer to at least run the numbers now is this: rates are locked by age and health at the time of application. If anything changes between now and next month — a prescription, a diagnosis, even a routine lab result — it can move where you qualify. The cost of waiting is usually invisible until it's not."  
**Pivot:** "Give me 5 minutes now. If it makes sense to move forward, we will. If not, you'll have the numbers for when you're ready."

### 3C — "Send Me an Email"
**Client:** "Just send me something in an email."  
**Acknowledge:** "I can send a summary."  
**Reframe:** "The challenge with email is I can't personalize it without your numbers. A generic quote doesn't tell you anything useful — it's just a rate without context. A 5-minute conversation lets me build something specific to your situation so what I send actually means something."  
**Pivot:** "Let me grab a few data points and I'll put together something based on your actual picture. What's your current household income?"

## Category 4: "I Have Coverage Through Work"

### 4A — Group Coverage Reliance
**Client:** "My employer gives me 2x salary."  
**Acknowledge:** "That's a solid foundation."  
**Reframe:** "2x salary covers roughly 24 months of income replacement. For a family with a mortgage and kids, the actual need is typically 10–15 years of income replacement plus debt payoff, plus education funding. The other issue: employer coverage ends when employment ends. No portability. If you leave, get laid off, or retire, that benefit disappears — and by then, your health and age may price you out of the private market."  
**Pivot:** "Let me show you the gap between what work provides and what your family actually needs. If it's small, you'll know."

### 4B — Supplemental Through Work
**Client:** "I bought the supplemental through work too."  
**Acknowledge:** "That's smart — most people skip that."  
**Reframe:** "Two things to check: First, the total — group base + supplemental — is it enough against the actual number? Second, the supplemental through your employer is still tied to your employment. It's convenient, but it's not portable. You're building your family's safety net on a platform you don't own."  
**Pivot:** "What's the total amount? Let me put it against your real number — mortgage, income, debts — and see where the gap lands."

## Category 5: Trust and Skepticism

### 5A — "I Don't Trust Insurance Companies"
**Client:** "I've heard too many horror stories."  
**Acknowledge:** "I get that — and some of it is earned."  
**Reframe:** "I work independently. I'm not captive to one carrier — I shop 30+ and show you the math. My job is to match your situation to the best-rated carrier for your health profile. The carriers I work with are A-rated, meaning their claims-paying ability is independently verified. But the real protection is transparency — I'll show you exactly what you're getting, what it costs, and what the alternatives are."  
**Pivot:** "What specifically concerns you? If there's a past experience driving it, I'd like to address it directly."

### 5B — "My Last Agent Was Terrible"
**Client:** "The last guy who called just wanted to sell me something."  
**Acknowledge:** "That's frustrating — and it happens more than it should."  
**Reframe:** "My approach is different. I start with your numbers, not a product. If the math doesn't show a gap, there's nothing to solve and I'll tell you that. Most of the time the gap is there and the last agent just didn't take the time to show it to you properly. I will."  
**Pivot:** "Let me walk you through the diagnostic. If it doesn't make sense, we stop. No pressure. Just data."

### 5C — "Insurance Is a Scam"
**Client:** "It's all designed to not pay out."  
**Acknowledge:** "I understand why people feel that way."  
**Reframe:** "Life insurance has a 99%+ claims-paid rate on in-force policies with the carriers I work with. The issues people hear about almost always involve lapsed policies, misrepresentation on the application, or contestability period disputes — all preventable. My job is to make sure you're properly placed, properly disclosed, and properly in force so that if something happens, the claim is clean."  
**Pivot:** "What would make you more comfortable? I can walk you through exactly how claims work with the carriers I recommend."

## Category 6: Price and Budget

### 6A — "I Can't Afford It"
**Client:** "That's way out of my budget."  
**Acknowledge:** "Budget matters — I'm not going to put you in something that creates a strain."  
**Reframe:** "Here's the tension: the gap is $400,000. The cost to close it is $47/month — that's $1.56/day. Without it, your family absorbs the full $400,000 liability. One of those numbers should concern you more than the other."  

*Hold the tension. Let the math sit.*  

**Pivot:** "That said, I have options at different levels. Let me show you both — the full architecture and the coverage-focused option — so you can see the trade-offs."

### 6B — "I'm Maxing Out My Retirement"
**Client:** "Every extra dollar goes to my 401k."  
**Acknowledge:** "That's disciplined — most people aren't that intentional."  
**Reframe:** "Here's the piece most advisors don't mention: your 401k is 100% exposed to market risk with no floor, taxed on the way out, penalized before 59½, and if something happens to you, your family inherits a tax liability — not tax-free liquidity. A properly designed policy creates an immediate estate that's income-tax-free to your beneficiaries, builds cash value with downside protection, and gives you access through tax-free policy loans. They're not competing — they solve different problems."  

*Compliance note: never guarantee returns or state that a policy is superior to a 401k. Position as a complementary planning vehicle.*  

**Pivot:** "I'm not saying change your retirement strategy. I'm saying there's a structural gap that your 401k doesn't cover — and the cost to close it is smaller than most people expect."

### 6C — "Let Me Check With My Financial Advisor"
**Client:** "I need to run this by my financial advisor."  
**Acknowledge:** "Good — that's a smart move."  
**Reframe:** "Most financial advisors focus on accumulation — 401k, IRA, brokerage. They rarely model what happens to the family's financial picture if income stops. That's not their lane. What I'll send you is the specific gap analysis so you can show your advisor exactly what we're solving and how it fits alongside everything else."  
**Pivot:** "Let me get the numbers finalized so you have something concrete to share. What's the best email for the summary?"

## Category 7: Cash Value and IUL-Specific Objections

### 7A — "Cash Value Policies Are a Rip-Off"
**Client:** "I've read that you should buy term and invest the difference."  
**Acknowledge:** "That's a popular philosophy — and there's a version of it that makes sense."  
**Reframe:** "The 'buy term and invest the difference' math assumes two things: that you actually invest the difference — most people don't — and that you never need permanent coverage. For families with estate planning needs, business succession, or a desire for tax-advantaged growth alongside protection, a properly designed cash value policy does things a brokerage account can't: income-tax-free death benefit, tax-free policy loans, downside floor protection, and living benefits. It's not either/or — it depends on the goal."  
**Pivot:** "Let me show you both options side by side with your numbers. Term alone and the full architecture. Then you decide which one solves what you're trying to solve."

### 7B — "IUL Fees Are Too High"
**Client:** "I've heard the fees inside these policies eat you alive."  
**Acknowledge:** "Some products do have higher internal costs — that's a fair concern."  
**Reframe:** "The question isn't the cost inside the policy — it's the net value after costs. A properly illustrated IUL with competitive internal charges still delivers tax-advantaged accumulation with a floor, tax-free access through loans, living benefits, and an income-tax-free death benefit. No other single vehicle does all of that. The illustration will show you the projected net value year by year — that's the number that matters."  
**Pivot:** "Let me run an illustration at your age and health class so you can see the actual numbers, not theoretical ones."

### 7C — "I'd Rather Invest in the Market"
**Client:** "I'll just put it in my brokerage account."  
**Acknowledge:** "Nothing wrong with that — market exposure has its place."  
**Reframe:** "Two differences worth considering: First, a brokerage account is 100% exposed. In a down year, your balance drops and there's no floor. A properly designed policy credits interest linked to index performance with a floor — you participate in gains and are protected from losses. Second, access: brokerage gains are taxable. Policy loans are not. And if something happens to you, a brokerage account passes through probate with potential tax implications. A death benefit is income-tax-free and immediate."  
**Pivot:** "It's not about replacing your investments. It's about building a second tier with different rules. Let me show you how they work together."

## Category 8: Retirement and Accumulation Objections

### 8A — "I'm Already Saving Enough"
**Client:** "Between my 401k and savings, I'm good."  
**Acknowledge:** "That's more than most people can say."  
**Reframe:** "The issue isn't the amount — it's the structure. All of that money lives in one tax bucket: taxable on withdrawal. If tax rates go up — and they historically have — your retirement income shrinks. A cash value policy creates a tax-diversified asset: tax-free access, tax-free death benefit, and it doesn't count against Social Security taxation thresholds. It's not about saving more — it's about saving smarter."  
**Pivot:** "Let me show you how adding a tax-free access layer changes your retirement income projection."

### 8B — "I'm Too Close to Retirement"
**Client:** "I'm 55 — it's too late for this."  
**Acknowledge:** "You'd be surprised how much the math still works."  
**Reframe:** "At 55, the focus shifts. You're not building 30 years of cash value — you're solving for three things: final expense coverage so your family isn't burdened, legacy planning so your estate transfers efficiently, and potentially living benefits as a long-term care alternative. The premium is higher, but the problems you're solving are immediate. And locking in coverage now, while you qualify, is the move — because at 60 or 65, a single health event can price you out entirely."  
**Pivot:** "Let me run the numbers for your age and health class. We'll see exactly what's available and what it costs."

### 8C — "My Financial Advisor Said No"
**Client:** "My advisor told me to never buy whole life or IUL."  
**Acknowledge:** "Advisors have strong opinions on this — and context matters."  
**Reframe:** "Most advisors are compensated on assets under management. A properly designed policy moves dollars off their platform. That's not a conspiracy — it's just an incentive structure worth understanding. The question isn't what your advisor recommends — it's whether your financial architecture has a gap that market-exposed, tax-deferred accounts can't solve. If the answer is yes, there's a planning conversation to have."  
**Pivot:** "I'll put together the analysis. You can share it with your advisor and make the decision together with full transparency."

## Category 9: Stalls and Delays

### 9A — "I Need to Think About It"
**Client:** "Let me think about it."  
**Acknowledge:** "I'd want to think about it too."  
**Reframe:** "There's usually one specific piece that's driving the hesitation — whether it's the premium, the coverage amount, or something about the process. If I can address that now, you'll have a clearer picture to think about."  
**Pivot:** "What's the main thing you're weighing?"

### 9B — "I Need to Talk to My Spouse"
**Client:** "My wife/husband needs to be part of this."  
**Acknowledge:** "Absolutely — this is a family decision."  
**Reframe:** "Here's what I'd suggest: I'll put together a summary with the options and the numbers so when you sit down together, you're looking at specifics, not generalities. Or — if they're available in the next 30 minutes, I can walk you both through it now and answer questions in real time."  
**Pivot:** "Are they available now, or should I prepare the summary and set a time for all three of us?"

### 9C — "Call Me Back Next Week"
**Client:** "I'm slammed. Call me next week."  
**Acknowledge:** "No problem."  
**Reframe:** "I'll lock in your numbers so when we reconnect, I can show you something specific instead of starting from scratch. Let me confirm a couple of data points before we hang up."  
**Pivot:** Move directly into demographics or income verification — partial commitment creates callback obligation.

## How to Run a Drill Session

1. Pick a category and drill number.
2. Leader reads the client line. Rep delivers the full ARP+ sequence from memory.
3. Group scores 1–10 on three criteria:
   - **Teach:** Did the reframe introduce new information?
   - **Tension:** Did the rep hold the uncomfortable truth, or rush to comfort?
   - **Pivot:** Did the redirect create forward motion?
4. Rotate. Everyone drills. Nobody watches from the sideline.
5. Assign the weakest sequence as homework.
