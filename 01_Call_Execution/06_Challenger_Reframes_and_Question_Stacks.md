# Challenger Reframes and Advanced Question Stacks

## How This Works

- A reframe is a **perspective shift** — not a rebuttal, not a correction, not a pitch.
- The goal is to put new information between the client and their current assumption so they **recalculate on their own**.
- Target response: "I never thought about it that way." If they say "I know" — you confirmed their existing belief. No value added.
- Each reframe in this bank includes: the client's belief, the new lens, and a follow-up question stack that deepens the shift.

---

## Section 1: Protection Gap Reframes

### 1.1 — "I Have Coverage Through Work"

**Client Belief:** My employer provides coverage. I'm taken care of.

**Reframe:**
"Employer coverage is a starting point — not a solution. It's typically 1–2x salary, which covers about 12–24 months of income replacement. For a family with a mortgage, debts, and dependents, the real need is 10–15x annual income. And it's not portable — if you leave, get laid off, or retire, the benefit disappears entirely. You're building your family's financial safety net on a platform you don't own and can't take with you."

**Follow-Up Stack:**
1. "What's the face amount on your group policy?"
2. "Is it portable — can you convert or take it with you if you leave?"
3. "What's the total liability picture — mortgage balance, debts, income replacement for 10 years, kids' education?"
4. "So we're looking at $[X] in need against $[Y] in group coverage. That's a $[gap] unhedged liability."

---

### 1.2 — "We Have Savings — We'd Be Fine"

**Client Belief:** Our savings provide a safety net.

**Reframe:**
"Savings buys time — it doesn't replace income. If I divide your savings by your monthly obligations — mortgage, debts, day-to-day expenses — it typically covers 3–6 months. After that, the surviving spouse is making structural decisions: sell the house, pull children from school, drain retirement accounts at tax penalties. Savings delays the crisis. It doesn't prevent it."

**Follow-Up Stack:**
1. "What does your liquid savings look like — checking, savings, emergency fund?"
2. "What are your total monthly obligations?"
3. "So that's roughly [X] months of runway before structural changes start?"
4. "After the savings are gone, what's the plan?"

---

### 1.3 — "I'm Young and Healthy — I'll Handle It Later"

**Client Belief:** I don't need to worry about this yet.

**Reframe:**
"Your health and age are assets right now — they qualify you for the best rate class at the lowest cost. Every year you wait, two things happen: the premium goes up, and one health event can move you from preferred to rated — or declined entirely. The economics of waiting are always worse. And the liabilities exist now. Your mortgage, your income dependency, your family's exposure — those don't wait for you to be ready."

**Follow-Up Stack:**
1. "What would a preferred rate at your age look like vs. what it costs at 45? 50?"
2. "If you had a routine lab result come back unexpected next year, how does that change your eligibility?"
3. "The protection gap exists today. What's the cost of leaving it open for another 5 years?"

---

### 1.4 — "My Policy Is Fine — I Haven't Looked at It in a While"

**Client Belief:** The old policy still works.

**Reframe:**
"When that policy was written, your income was different, your mortgage was different, and your family may have looked completely different. A policy written for a $180K income and a $200K mortgage doesn't cover a $260K income and a $380K mortgage. Most people are insured for a version of their life that no longer exists."

**Follow-Up Stack:**
1. "When was the policy written?"
2. "What was your income at the time? What is it now?"
3. "Was the mortgage the same? Kids planned or already here?"
4. "Let me run the current numbers against the original design. We'll see if it holds up."

## Section 2: Wealth and Accumulation Reframes

### 2.1 — "I'm Maxing Out My 401k — That's My Plan"

**Client Belief:** Retirement savings is the only wealth-building strategy I need.

**Reframe:**
"A 401k does one thing well — tax-deferred accumulation with employer match. But it has structural constraints: it's 100% exposed to market risk with no floor, it's taxed as ordinary income on the way out, penalized before 59½, and if something happens to you, your family inherits a tax liability — not tax-free liquidity. A properly designed cash value policy solves different problems: downside protection with a floor, tax-free access through policy loans, living benefits if your health changes, and an income-tax-free death benefit. They're not competing — they occupy different positions in a diversified financial architecture."

*Compliance: Never state a policy outperforms a 401k. Position as complementary. Reference carrier illustrations for projected values.*

**Follow-Up Stack:**
1. "What percentage of your retirement assets are in pre-tax accounts?"
2. "What tax rate do you expect to pay when you start withdrawing?"
3. "If the market drops 30% two years before you retire — sequence-of-returns risk — what happens to your timeline?"
4. "Do you have any assets that give you tax-free access in retirement? Roth? Policy loans?"
5. "What if there were a way to add a tax-free income layer without reducing your 401k contributions?"

---

### 2.2 — "I'd Rather Invest in Real Estate / the Market"

**Client Belief:** I can get better returns elsewhere.

**Reframe:**
"I'm not suggesting you stop investing. Real estate and market investments serve accumulation. But they don't solve three problems: immediate estate creation at death (dollar one, day one), tax-free liquidity for your family without selling assets or waiting for markets, and living benefits if your health changes while you're still alive. A life insurance policy is the only financial instrument that creates all three simultaneously. It's not either/or — it's architectural."

**Follow-Up Stack:**
1. "If something happened tomorrow, how quickly could your family access liquidity from your real estate or brokerage accounts?"
2. "What are the tax implications of liquidating those positions under pressure?"
3. "Is there a way to protect the assets you're building without depending on market conditions at the worst possible time?"

---

### 2.3 — "Cash Value Policies Are a Rip-Off"

**Client Belief:** Buy term and invest the difference.

**Reframe:**
"That strategy works in theory — if you actually invest the difference every month for 20+ years, which data shows most people don't. And it assumes you'll never need permanent coverage. For clients with estate planning needs, business succession, or a desire for tax-advantaged growth alongside permanent protection, a properly designed policy does things a brokerage account doesn't: income-tax-free death benefit, tax-free policy loans, downside floor protection, living benefits. It's not about which one is 'better' — it's about which problems you're trying to solve."

**Follow-Up Stack:**
1. "If you bought a 20-year term, what happens at year 21? You're uninsured at 55 with a different health profile."
2. "Have you consistently invested the difference, or is that theoretical?"
3. "What problems do you need solved: protection only, or protection plus accumulation plus legacy?"

---

### 2.4 — "Insurance Isn't an Investment"

**Client Belief:** I shouldn't use insurance to build wealth.

**Reframe:**
"You're right — and I'd never position it as an investment. It's a planning vehicle. The distinction matters. An investment is designed to maximize return at some level of risk. A properly designed policy is designed to create guarantees: a guaranteed death benefit, a guaranteed floor on cash value, guaranteed access through loans, and guaranteed living benefits. It's the foundation layer — not the growth layer. It does what your investments can't: create certainty in an uncertain plan."

**Follow-Up Stack:**
1. "What role does certainty play in your overall financial plan?"
2. "Is there anywhere in your current architecture that provides guaranteed liquidity — no market risk, no tax on access, no penalties?"
3. "What if the foundation of your plan was guaranteed, and everything on top of it could take more risk?"

## Section 3: Legacy and Generational Reframes

### 3.1 — "I Don't Need to Leave Anything Behind"

**Client Belief:** My kids will be fine on their own.

**Reframe:**
"This isn't about dependency — it's about efficiency. Without a death benefit, your estate passes through probate, potentially gets taxed, and your family waits months for access. With a properly structured death benefit, they receive income-tax-free cash within weeks — no probate, no tax event, immediate liquidity. The question isn't whether your kids can survive without it. It's whether you want the transfer to be clean or chaotic."

**Follow-Up Stack:**
1. "What does your current estate plan look like — will, trust, beneficiary designations?"
2. "If you passed tomorrow, how long before your family has access to liquid funds?"
3. "What's the cost of probate in your state? How does that compare to the premium of a policy that bypasses it entirely?"

---

### 3.2 — "I'll Just Leave Them the House"

**Client Belief:** Real estate is my legacy.

**Reframe:**
"Real estate is an illiquid asset with carrying costs. Property taxes, maintenance, insurance, and potentially a remaining mortgage continue after you're gone. Your family inherits the asset and the liability. If they need cash — for funeral costs, debts, income replacement — they're selling under pressure, often below market value. A death benefit creates the liquidity to keep the house or sell it on their terms. It's not a replacement for the real estate — it's the cash that makes the inheritance work."

**Follow-Up Stack:**
1. "What's the remaining mortgage on the property?"
2. "What are the annual carrying costs — taxes, insurance, maintenance?"
3. "If your family needed $50K in cash within 30 days, where does it come from without selling?"

---

### 3.3 — "My Kids Are Adults — They Don't Need a Death Benefit"

**Client Belief:** Adult children don't need financial protection from parents.

**Reframe:**
"Adult children inherit adult problems. Final expenses — $15K–$25K on average. Outstanding debts or a reverse mortgage. Lost time from work for estate management. Tax implications if they inherit pre-tax retirement accounts. A death benefit doesn't replace your parenting — it prevents your passing from becoming a financial burden on the people you raised. The difference between a planned transition and an unplanned one is preparation."

**Follow-Up Stack:**
1. "What are your end-of-life costs likely to be — funeral, medical, estate administration?"
2. "Do your adult children know where everything is — accounts, debts, documents?"
3. "Would you rather they deal with a clean check or six months of estate resolution?"

## Section 4: Living Benefits and Health Reframes

### 4.1 — "If I Get Sick, I Have Health Insurance"

**Client Belief:** Health insurance covers everything.

**Reframe:**
"Health insurance covers medical bills. It doesn't cover the income you stop earning. It doesn't cover the mortgage while you're recovering. It doesn't cover the cost of modifying your home for long-term care or hiring help while you can't work. Living benefits on a life insurance policy give you access to a portion of your death benefit — tax-free — if you're diagnosed with a qualifying chronic, critical, or terminal condition. It's not replacing health insurance. It's covering everything health insurance doesn't."

**Follow-Up Stack:**
1. "If you were diagnosed with something that kept you out of work for 12 months, what happens to your household income?"
2. "Does your disability coverage, if any, replace 100% of your income? Or a fraction?"
3. "What would tax-free access to $100K–$200K mean during a medical crisis — without adding to your medical debt?"

---

### 4.2 — "I Don't Want to Pay for Something I'll Never Use"

**Client Belief:** I'm healthy and won't need it.

**Reframe:**
"Nobody plans to use their airbags either. But here's the math: 1 in 4 adults will experience a disability before age 67. Cancer, heart disease, and stroke are the leading causes of claims. A policy with living benefits covers you three ways — chronic illness, critical illness, and terminal diagnosis — while the death benefit remains intact if none of those happen. You're not paying for something you'll never use. You're paying for the right to access capital under the worst possible circumstances."

**Follow-Up Stack:**
1. "Do you have any long-term care coverage or disability insurance currently?"
2. "If you couldn't work for 6 months due to a health event, what's your plan for covering household expenses?"
3. "Would knowing you had access to $[X] tax-free during a medical event change how you think about the premium?"

## Section 5: Retirement Efficiency Reframes

### 5.1 — "I'm Already Saving Enough for Retirement"

**Client Belief:** My savings rate is sufficient.

**Reframe:**
"The amount isn't the only variable — the structure matters. If 100% of your retirement is in pre-tax accounts, every dollar withdrawn is taxed as ordinary income. If tax rates increase — and with national debt levels, many planners expect they will — your retirement income shrinks. A cash value policy creates a tax-free access layer: policy loans don't trigger income tax, don't show up on your tax return, and don't affect your Social Security taxation threshold. It's not about saving more. It's about creating diversification across tax treatments."

**Follow-Up Stack:**
1. "What percentage of your retirement is in pre-tax accounts?"
2. "What tax bracket do you expect in retirement — and how confident are you about that?"
3. "If you could access $500K in retirement tax-free, how would that change your withdrawal strategy?"

---

### 5.2 — "I'm Too Old for This to Make Sense"

**Client Belief:** It's too late to start a policy.

**Reframe:**
"At 55 or 60, the conversation changes — but it doesn't end. The focus shifts from 30-year accumulation to three specific problems: final expense coverage so your family isn't burdened with $15K–$25K in costs, legacy planning so your estate transfers efficiently, and potentially a living benefit policy as a long-term care alternative — which is significantly less expensive than standalone LTC insurance. And locking in coverage now, while you qualify, is the move. At 65, one health event can make you uninsurable."

**Follow-Up Stack:**
1. "What's your current health status — any conditions that might affect eligibility?"
2. "Do you have a long-term care plan? Who pays if you need care for 3–5 years?"
3. "What does your estate plan look like for the transfer of assets — is it clean?"

## How to Use This Bank

1. **In Training:** Pick a reframe. Rep delivers it from memory. Group scores: Did it teach? Was there a perspective shift?
2. **In Live Prep:** Before a callback, the rep identifies which client belief they're likely to face and rehearses the corresponding reframe + follow-up stack.
3. **In QA:** Score the rep's reframe on the call. Did they teach or argue? Did they follow up with the stack or drop the thread?
4. **In Coaching:** When a rep can't move a client past a belief, assign the specific reframe from this bank. Drill until natural.

---

## Rules

1. A reframe teaches. If it argues, it's a rebuttal — rewrite it.
2. Every reframe ends with a follow-up stack. The reframe opens the door. The questions walk through it.
3. Compliance: No guaranteed returns. No "better than" comparisons. Position as planning vehicle. Reference carrier illustrations for projected values.
4. Constructive tension: Hold the uncomfortable truth. Don't rush to resolve it.
5. Specificity: Use the client's numbers, names, and situation. Generic reframes bounce off.
